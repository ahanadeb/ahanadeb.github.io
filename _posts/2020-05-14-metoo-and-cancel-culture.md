\
\
When the #metoo movement began, it began as a movement of gender rights, of justice, the ever echoing call to break down the hierarchy of gender and power. From R. Kelly to Harvey Weinstein, #metoo called to bring down men who could not be held accountable in any way other than vigilante justice. This part is important but let’s put this aside for a while now. 

\
\
In JU, what we owe to #metoo and call-out culture is the environment that it has tried to create, to allow those abused to come forward and openly talk about the issues and problems in the campus. I doubt the Google Drive incident could've come out so openly and strongly if it weren't for the waves of #metoo that had pushed the boundaries of our understanding of abuse, repeatedly. It taught us that rapists are not necessarily the stereotypical men who hide in bushes and hold you at gunpoint, they are people who walk among us, are even nice to us. We have actively dismissed the argument “but he never did this with me, so…”, and I can’t emphasise how important this was. There are lot more barriers to break, and a lot more for us to learn. I can go on, but the positives of this movement have been written and spoken about again and again (I’ll leave some links of my favorite books/articles down below if you want to read), but there are things which haven't yet been brought to light as clearly. Gender discrimination is as old as time itself, but the internet is new, this form of the movement is new, therefore its criticism is also new. I’ll lean on my Mommy to explain this, so let’s get to the problems that are happening right now. 

\
\
There are essentially two types of popular arguments that you’ll find on the internet over this- one group claiming that the call-out culture has ruined intimacy and flirting, it has gone “too far”, men can't even talk to women anymore, and something needs to be done to stop this nonsense. The other group of feminists and allies claiming that the movement is finally bringing accountability to powerful ‘abusers’ and your fear is your admission of guilt. Call me a centrist, but none of these truly grasp the nuance of gender discriminations and consequently the nuances of the movements associated with it.

\
\
When I started to write this down, I was scared of attacking certain beliefs and institutions, I was afraid of a backlash. But why?

 No movement has been spared criticism, so why do I feel so afraid of criticizing this one? That is precisely my first criticism. We’ve been throwing labels like ‘enabler’, ‘abuser’, ‘MRA’ so frivolously and recklessly, that the terms which carry much significance, lose its weight in case of serious offences. I feel afraid of being told that I’m seeking “male validation” while critiquing a movement that talks of the oppression I face. All of my criticism about the movement mainly focuses on the traps that I have myself fallen into, and I will admit, I have been blinded by the reactionary politics around this. Which brings me to my first point. 

\
\
ABSTRACTION

The term you've heard often is “enabler” and “abuser”. The allegations that these terms carry are severe and meaningful. If you take a look at the Google Drive case, the people who initiated it, and/or perpetuated the activities, undoubtedly deserve these labels. But are all the allegations of the same magnitude?

\
\
Let’s say a person asks someone to sext with them unprompted in 2017, and then gets blocked? Creepy right. I’ve been there on the other side of this. And to the women reading this, I’m sure this is not an uncommon experience for you too. The action of the person in question is undoubtedly abusive. But does this call for the person to be termed an “abuser”? I’ve seen posts like these calling out people as “toxic”, “abusive”, “predator”, and my first speculations about the specifics are always pretty bad. Are we absolutely sure that there is no space for reformation of this person, no space for education? Aren’t these blanket statements a bit too vague? Should these heavy labels be used indiscriminately on everyone? 

\
\
Another popular trope is “enabling”. Oh, how hard I had fallen for this myself. We have all sat with people who have made sexist jokes, said repulsive things about women, and no matter what image of yourself you project on your social media account, you too have slut-shamed someone or the other at some point in your life. And it's really not your fault. We have been brought up in a system where slut-shaming, competing with, and demeaning other women for their femininity and choices are INCENTIVISED. This behavior has sedimented inside our brains for long, and we are all trying to unlearn it. Is it fair, to drag every single friend of the “accused” down the same hole as the accused themselves, without giving them a chance at retribution, self-reflection, and analysis? Are they(we) all really equally as guilty as the perpetrators of the Google-Drive case to receive the same disproportional response? What was the point of “calling out” these enablers ? Did they learn from their mistakes? Did they grow as people? Or did we just shove them into a hole of account-deactivation and self-isolation?

\
\
MOTIVE

The personal is political, so I cannot leave myself completely removed in the objective sideline. A lot of my friends have called out the people who have abused and exploited them, and since I’ve known them and their account on a very personal level, I can tell you their motivation to call-out was perfectly genuine. I’m sure you've had a similar experience as mine or my friends. 

\
\
But is that always the case? Let’s take a look at the fall of a very popular couple, (I do not personally know them) R & S. Absolute social butterflies, vocal intersectional feminists, whatnot. And apparent from the screenshots, had engaged in pretty fucked up activities. Now I’m no one to tell you whether they are capable of reparations or not. Maybe they’re repeat offenders, who knows. But take a closer look at the comment section. You cannot overlook the fact that a multitude of people who were slandering them, were not motivated by the slightest sense of moral integrity. It was about a fall, a spectacle - what initially appeared like the guillotine, was actually like one, a source of perverted and sadistic fascination. 

\
\
How many of us really want to see the improvement of these people? How many of us want an actual apology and move forward towards creating a more coherent narrative in this fight? I do not have the answers. You have to ask yourself.

\
\
SO WHAT DO WE REALLY WANT FROM THE ACCUSED?

Let’s say you (yes, YOU) have been called out as an ‘abuser’ or an ‘enabler’. I don’t know how sincere it is because I don’t know you, and this pitfall is a HUGE POINT OF CONCERN. No one knows. What can you do at this point?

\
\
Here are three things you can do, and it’ll still never be appropriate for the internet. 

a) Apologize- Once you apologize right away, with or without realizing what you did wrong, or even taking the time to understand if you did anything wrong at all, your apology will be tagged as insincere, and a “manipulative attempt” to coerce the victim into forgiving you.

\
\
b) Try to explain your position - oops, your timeline is now a dumpster fire. You will be accused of being incapable of self-introspection, of invalidating the trauma of the victim, when you didn’t even get a chance to explain your position publicly. 

\
\
c) Stay silent- now I totally wanted to make a graph of account deactivations in Kolkata in the past one week, but I’m lazy. If you deactivate or refuse to comment immediately, there can be a lot of reasons for that. Maybe you’re thinking of a proper way of response, or maybe you're trying to understand exactly what went wrong. Whatever it may be, you'll immediately be accused of fleeing accountability, and gaslighting the victim. 

\
\
We fall into this trap that an instant apology is more sincere than a delayed one. I know for a fact that I indulged in this delusion. It takes time to understand where you went wrong, to self-introspect, to get over the overwhelming nature of the accusations, and respond and improve yourself. It can take more than days. 

\
\
What we do by expecting immediate unconditional apologies is stifle an environment encouraging self-improvement, and this one is probably the most dangerous, we encourage insincere pointless apologies from people who in reality deserve no forgiveness for the heinousness of their crime. We snub people capable of change and forgive those incapable of such. 

\
\
Needlessly to say I absolutely believe that there are people who are so harmful to those around them, that they do not deserve a voice anywhere, let alone forgiveness. So what should we be doing about them? Which brings us to

\
\
JUSTICE

So that thing we put aside, about Harvey Weinstein? Let’s bring that back. What #metoo promised was to bring down men who were revered, above all criticism, and considered to be beyond law and accountability. #metoo wanted to bring justice to victims that were impossible to attain at least initially through any due process. Is that really the case here? Besides an obvious few accused, others simply do not have that social capital, public relation agents defending them, are not insanely rich and do not have contacts that we understand are imperative for social survival. So are we kicking people out of orbit without due process? People who we can conventionally bring to justice?

\
\
One problem that I have faced while discussing this is the problem of the statistics. “Most sexual abuse accounts are true”. Given the context of this country and our personal experiences, you really don't need a number to make that comment. It is invariably true. Facebook is the tip of the iceberg when it comes to the struggle in the lives of women and non-binary folks. But from what I understand, #metoo is a movement of liberation, a fight for equality, a call for dismantling the power structure. This is not a war against people who are capable of change. This is not a war where we can isolate and abandon people without any due process, or any regards for their rights, and declare it is collateral damage for a greater cause. This is the greater cause. Changing people, reforming human beings, this is the greater cause. The current fight at least how I see it has shifted from this principle axis that it started with. 

\
\
If you ask yourself and the people around you how they think they can bring any solution to this, the most common answer that you're likely to get is “sensitization”. We need to educate kids, teachers, and parents, about consent, bodily autonomy, sexual orientation, identity, gender roles, etc. We should be taking immediate efforts towards this, but I hate to admit it, it is a LONG process. Reformation is tedious, abuse is real and immediate. So how do we go about this?

\
\
As I see things now, no matter how much you want to abolish the State and establish the new world order, the law does have the capability to bring some sort of justice to those who are dangerous if left without intervention. Yes, the process is tedious, legal harassment is real. There are tonnes of violating questions and tests that a victim might have to go through before their innocence is even considered. Like the two-finger-test. I’m not gonna describe how brutally violating it is, but through the efforts of people like you and me, who are seeking justice, in 2013 in the case of Lilu @ Rajesh and anr v. State of Haryana, the two-finger-test was declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court (read here). Similarly, through the LGBTQIAA+ movements, and repeated protests and fights homosexuality was decriminalized as well (read here). Our fights for our rights are very very far from being over, and you can say that these are very small stepping stones in that journey, but I will still advocate that these steps are important. They have real-life and immediate implications. 

\
\
Social media is an outlet for our grievances with the system, the hierarchies, and the bureaucracies, but the fight beyond that remains imperative. When we can bring these perpetrators who are not really above law, to justice through the hands of law itself, wouldn't we be creating a better narrative? A chance for everyone to avail of justice? Shouldn’t we be fighting tooth and nail against the laws that are rigged against us, against our humanity and rights to fair treatment? I can only appeal to you to fight and nothing more, I can only assure you that we will fight together.

\
\
WHY I DIDN’T CALL OUT 

This is the part where I need to clear out something very personal. I think that being able to open up about the abuse that you have faced or still do, it a very important step towards justice. What #metoo has done is it has opened to us the dimensions of abuse, and I learn more and more about it every day. Starting this discussion is a very brave thing to do, but I found it to be an utterly harassing experience to be so vulnerable so openly, just to demand justice that I really deserve. Just take a look at the posts of the victims who have opened up. Almost every single one of them face a tremendous amount of abuse and harassment after calling someone out, and the entire thing doesn’t even move a single step towards providing them justice. Why aren’t we fighting for a system where people can fight for their rights without having to bare their most traumatizing moments in front of the most heartless crowd on earth? How can a system so dehumanising for everyone involved, be a solution?

\
\
During the recent “discourse” over justice and social media trails, for reasons that I have been trying to explain so far, I have absolutely lost faith that I can avail of any form of justice through this tool. Social media shaming is a horrifying thing, which I don't think I or the person in question deserves. It would be a permanent scar because remember, nothing disappears off the internet, the screenshots and accounts will keep circulating and it would end up being a traumatizing experience for both of us, without a single push towards self-improvement or actualization. I’d, of course, be slut-shamed by some, my narrative twisted and needlessly politicized and weaponized by others when all I wanted is for both of us to be better people, to create a healthy environment for discussion and understanding. 

\
\
While I believe that the actions of the person in question were truly wrong, it is still a huge leap from criticizing the person’s action to criticizing the person themselves, a leap I’m not willing to make. Whatever accusation I throw, irrespective of the severity of it, will be oversimplified and misappropriated by labeling them as “abuser”, “predator” and whatnot, with absolutely no acceptance for his side of the story. What if I have been emotionally abusive? That's not far off. What if I have been manipulative? Even if you extend me immense trust that I am not, I can assure you no amount of honesty and humility will make me admit to my own mistakes as openly when I ax him for his. And why will I be worthy of this trust in the first place? What if I’m someone you don't know? Or someone you don’t like? By now, this is a real possibility. How can I appeal for justice based on the trust I have “earned” through my social capital, how is that just for the person I’m accusing? How is that just for you, as a third party, to be pushed into accepting my account as gospel, and shun someone you barely know into a dark hole of rejection? 

\
\
THE PROBLEM IS BIGGER THAN THAT

Why I felt the need to say all of this is that the dumpster fire that Facebook is right now, what we are doing is actively escalating conflict instead of promoting any way to come to a mutual understanding and fight together for justice. We are creating respective echo chambers for feminists and men’s rights activists, and everyone who does not agree to our exact brand of feminism, we push them outside to fend for themselves. Someone who has his life thrown into disarray for a crime he didn’t even understand that he committed since we gave him no space for introspection and discussion, which echo chamber do you think he’ll find himself in? Is that what we want? 

\
\
I’m not misdirecting my anger, I’m not calling any feminism “imperfect” or “pseudo”. I’m trying to appeal to you, to reconsider and reevaluate what exactly we are fighting for, and why we have shifted. Needless to say, call-out culture is effective when it comes to men who are so powerful and harmful, that letting them speak is an assault to the idea of democracy itself. But in a temporary, reactionary attitude to reprimand these people, we’re potentially ruining lives which could've been bettered, and more importantly derailing a movement which is crucial and can effectively bring exactly criminals like these to justice. 

\
\
Movements are meant to be moulded through criticism and introspection. But it seems impossible now to challenge narratives and expect any respectful engagement, and that’s precisely what I want to change. 

For example, I find the phrase “Believe women” very misleading. At least how it’s used conventionally. In a country like India, where women are judged, slut-shamed, raped, blamed for it, and then shoved in a corner of disbelief, this phrase is very crucial. What this implies is that an overwhelming majority of violence against women is real, and a huge percentage of it is disbelieved simply because they are not men. Believe women means don't disbelieve someone because they are women, it does NOT mean believe everyone who is a woman.

\
\
We have to understand, no matter how overwhelmingly true the accounts may be, “believe women” when set as a norm, will be very easy to abuse. 

\
\
You can tell me I’m wrong, correct me, and engage, I’m willing to learn, but how many of are up for that effort? There’s a particular amount of comfort in just labeling me an “enabler”, a “sympathizer”, etc, and just shut out everything I tried to challenge. The difficult part is self-introspection, to identify where we are going wrong, to question everything, EVERYTHING.

\
\
As you had promised you have passed me the mic. How does it matter if no one is willing to listen?
